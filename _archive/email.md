Strait of Malacca:

The world's busiest and most economically vital waterway, the Strait of Malacca is at the heart of the Indo-Pacific economy. Each year, approximately 40% of global maritime trade flows through the narrow passage, with much of it stopping over at the Port of Singapore, the world's second-busiest port facility. With a width of approximately 2 miles at its narrowest and a depth of 82 feet at its shallowest, the strait has grown increasingly crowded over time, raising concerns about its future viability.

Potential Threats:

Overcrowding
Piracy
Illicit Trade

Taiwan Strait:

Stretching for approximately 230 miles from North to South, the Taiwan Strait forms the boundary between the People's Republic of China and the Republic of China (Taiwan). While the strait has long been a crucial artery for container ships bound for China, Japan and the Korean Peninsula, it has grown increasingly unsettled in recent decades as the PRC has escalated its military activity in the vicinity of Taiwan. Nevertheless, the waterway accounts for approximately 20% of global maritime trade by value.

Potential Threats:

Geopolitical Conflict
Blockades

Strait of Hormuz:

The sole maritime connection between the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean, the Strait Hormuz is a critical node in the global petroleum supply chain. For decades, huge volumes of Iranian oil have flowed through the narrow passage, with over 20 million barrels a day transiting in 2024, accounting for approximately 20% of global petroleum consumption. As tensions between Iran and the United States have mounted, however, the strait has become an increasingly contested space.

Potential Threats:

Geopolitical Conflict
State-Sponsored Seizures
Piracy

Luzon Strait:

A key waterway separating Taiwan from the Philippines, the Luzon Strait serves as a crucial link in regional and transpacific trade networks. Large percentages of goods bound for the United States and other ports in the Americas transit the strait on a daily basis, while Japan relies heavily on the passage for energy imports. As tensions between the People's Republic of China and the Republic of China (Taiwan) have risen, the strait has also emerged as a key geopolitical faultline.

Potential Threats:

Geopolitical Conflicts
Blockades

Lombok Strait:

Stretching between the Indonesian islands of Lombok and Bali, the Lombok Strait has risen in economic importance in recent years. Far deeper than the Strait of Malacca, the passage as become an increasingly popular alternate route for the largest container ships. The Indonesian government has worked to encourage this shift by expanding local infrastructure. As it has grown in significance, however, the Lombok Strait has seen increased threats from piracy and environmental degradation.

Potential Threats:

Piracy
Environmental Degradation
Illicit Trade

Bab el-Mandeb:

A crucial gateway linking the Indian Ocean and the Red Sea (and thus the Suez Canal), the Bab el-Mandeb is among the world's foremost trade corridors. Due to its proximity to the oil-producing nations of the Middle East, the passage plays a key role in maritime petroleum supply chains, with large percentages of global oil exports flowing through it. However, regional conflicts have significantly threatened the stability of the strait, as Houthi militias based in Yemen have launched repeated attacks on commercial vessels.

Potential Threats:

Piracy
Extremist Violence
Geopolitical Conflict
