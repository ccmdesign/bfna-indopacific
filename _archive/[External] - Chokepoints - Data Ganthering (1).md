# Indo-Pacific Maritime Chokepoints Infographic

## Project Overview for BFNA

**Prepared by:** CCM Design  
**Date:** January 2026

---

## The Concept

We're creating an interactive infographic that visualizes how goods flow through the most critical shipping lanes in the Indo-Pacific—the narrow straits that global trade depends on.

The piece will allow users to explore ship traffic over time, understand what's being transported (oil, containers, bulk goods), and grasp why these waterways matter geopolitically. A timeline slider will let viewers see how traffic patterns shift across seasons and in response to global events.

---

## The Five Chokepoints

We'll focus on five Indo-Pacific straits:

| Strait | Why It Matters |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Strait of Malacca** | The big one—nearly a quarter of all global seaborne trade passes between Malaysia and Indonesia |
| **Taiwan Strait** | Half the world's container fleet transits here; central to any Taiwan scenario planning |
| **Strait of Hormuz** | The world's oil tap—20% of global oil and a third of LNG flows through this narrow gap |
| **Lombok Strait** | The backup route for large tankers if Malacca becomes blocked or congested |
| **Luzon Strait** | Gateway between the South China Sea and Pacific; critical for Japan and Korea |

## Our Data Approach

### The Challenge

Real-time cargo tracking data is expensive—commercial providers charge $50,000 to $200,000+ per year because commodity traders pay for early signals.

### Our Solution

We're building a custom dataset by combining multiple free, authoritative sources:

**1\. Ship Traffic Data**  
The UK Office for National Statistics publishes weekly ship crossing counts for several major straits, broken down by vessel type (tankers, container ships, cargo vessels). This gives us 2+ years of time-series data for the Taiwan Strait and Strait of Hormuz.

**2\. Cargo Volume Estimates**  
The U.S. Energy Information Administration tracks oil and LNG flows through global chokepoints. UNCTAD (the UN's trade body) publishes comprehensive seaborne trade statistics. By combining ship counts with these official cargo figures, we can estimate what's actually moving through each strait.

**3\. Trade Flow Context**  
Research from CSIS and other institutions has mapped bilateral trade relationships onto shipping routes—helping us understand not just how many ships, but where goods are coming from and going to.

### What We Can Show

- Weekly/monthly ship traffic by vessel type  
- Estimated oil and LNG volumes (barrels per day)  
- Seasonal patterns and year-over-year trends  
- Impact of disruptions (e.g., Red Sea crisis rerouting)

### What We Cannot Show (Without Commercial Data)

- Exact cargo contents of individual ships  
- Real-time tracking  
- Precise origin-destination pairs for every vessel

## Data Transparency

The infographic will include a short methodology note explaining:

- Which government and international sources we used  
- How we estimated cargo volumes from ship counts  
- Any limitations or confidence levels in the data

This is important for BFNA's credibility—the piece should feel like original research, not just recycled statistics.

---

## Timeline Considerations

- **UK ONS data** covers January 2022 through April 2024  
- **EIA/UNCTAD data** is typically annual, with some lag  
- We can supplement with additional sources as they become available

If a longer time horizon is needed, we could begin collecting real-time data now (using free ship tracking APIs) to extend the dataset going forward.
